// SANGRE DE CRISTO MOUNTAINS — DAWN OF THE HANGING //

You crest the ridge just as the first pale light touches the peaks. Below, the town of Crow's Perch clings to the mountainside like a miner's lantern hooked onto a cliff — narrow streets, steep-roofed buildings, and a single bell tower that cuts a dark silhouette against the morning sky. Pines crowd the upper slopes. Somewhere below, a stream rushes over stone.

You've ridden three days from Santa Fe, chasing a rumor that won't let you sleep. A man named Santiago Valdez is scheduled to hang at dawn today — convicted of poisoning the town's water with silver tailings, causing a sickness that has claimed eleven children. But the letter you received last month, unsigned and written in a hurried hand, said: "Valdez is innocent. The governor did it. Valdez knows where the Madrigal gold is buried. If he dies, the truth dies with him. Come quickly."

Below, you can make out three points of interest. The gallows stands on a bare knoll at the northern edge of town — you can see a small crowd already gathering. The marshal's office sits on Main Street, its windows lit with oil lamps. And the Silver Vein Saloon, the largest building in town, spills piano music and yellow light into the cold morning air. You have maybe an hour before the hanging. Maybe less.

// The air is thin and cold at this altitude. The pines smell like winter. Somewhere in these mountains, Spanish gold has been hidden for three hundred years. Somewhere in this town, a man who knows where it is waits to die. //


            // GALLOWS KNOLL — THE HOUR APPROACHES //
            

                
The gallows is a crude but sturdy structure — fresh pine beams, a trapdoor, a single noose swaying gently in the mountain breeze. About thirty townsfolk have gathered, their faces a mixture of grief, anger, and grim satisfaction. A preacher reads from a worn Bible. A woman in a black shawl weeps silently near the front.


                
On the platform, Santiago Valdez stands with his hands bound. He's younger than you expected — maybe twenty-five, with tired dark eyes and the look of a man who hasn't slept in weeks. His gaze sweeps the crowd and lands on you. Something flickers in his expression. Recognition? Hope? Or just the desperate scanning of a condemned man looking for anyone who might help?


                
A heavyset man in an expensive wool coat stands near the marshal — Governor Silas Crane, his presence unexpected at a small-town hanging. He watches the proceedings with the patient satisfaction of a man who has already won. Behind him, two armed guards in territorial uniforms stand at attention.


                
// The marshal checks his pocket watch. The preacher closes his Bible. You have minutes, not hours. //

            

            

                
                
            

        


        
        

            

            // MARSHAL'S OFFICE — MAIN STREET — THE LAW'S SIDE //
            

                
The marshal's office is small and cluttered — wanted posters on the walls, a rifle rack by the door, a potbelly stove glowing in the corner. Marshal John Whitfield is a gaunt man in his fifties, with deep-set eyes and the careful demeanor of someone who has learned that the law and justice are not always the same thing. He sizes you up as you enter.


                
"You're the rider from Santa Fe," he says. It's not a question. "Word travels fast in a town this size. You're here about Valdez, I take it." He pours two cups of coffee and slides one across the desk. "I don't like this hanging any more than you do. But the evidence was... compelling. Silver tailings in the well. Valdez's tools found nearby. And the governor himself pushed for a swift trial." He pauses, lowering his voice. "Between you and me, something doesn't sit right. But I'm one man with a badge against the territorial government. What exactly are you hoping to find here?"


                
He leans back. "I can deputize you — give you legal authority to investigate. Or I can tell you what I know about the Apache connection. Chief Nantan's people have been watching the town for weeks. They say the sickness started when Crane's men began surveying for the railroad. They say the governor is poisoning the land to drive everyone out."


                
// Marshal Whitfield is tired. He wants to do the right thing. He just doesn't know what it is anymore. //

            

            

                
                
            

        


        
        

            

            // SILVER VEIN SALOON — MAIN STREET — WHISKEY & WHISPERS //
            

                
The Silver Vein is warm and dim, smelling of pine smoke, spilled whiskey, and old secrets. At this early hour, only the serious drinkers remain — men with nowhere else to be and women who've learned to listen more than they speak. A tired piano player picks out a melancholy tune in the corner. Behind the bar, a wiry man with graying hair polishes glasses and watches everything.


                
Two figures catch your attention. At a table near the back, a young woman in traveling clothes sits alone, a half-empty glass before her. She has the look of someone who's been waiting for something — or someone — for a long time. When she sees you, her eyes narrow with something between suspicion and hope.


                
At the bar, three men in territorial uniforms are playing poker — Governor Crane's men, from the look of their insignias. One of them, a sergeant with a broken nose, is loudly recounting a story about "the vein we found up near Spider Rock" before another soldier elbows him into silence. A map and a small leather journal sit on the bar beside their chips.


                
// The woman wants to talk. The soldiers want to play cards. Both paths lead to information — but the kind of information you get depends on who you approach first. //

            

            

                
                
            

        


        
        

            

            // GALLOWS KNOLL — THE TRAP DROPS //
            

                
The trapdoor slams open. Santiago Valdez falls. The rope catches with a sound that echoes off the mountains. The crowd exhales — some in relief, some in horror. The woman in the black shawl collapses to her knees. Governor Crane watches without expression, then turns and walks briskly toward a waiting carriage, his business apparently concluded.


                
In the confusion that follows, you notice something: a small leather pouch has fallen from Valdez's vest during the hanging. It lies in the dust beneath the gallows, unnoticed by the dispersing crowd. The marshal is busy directing the body's removal. The governor's carriage is already rolling down Main Street. You have a narrow window.


                
When you retrieve the pouch, you find inside a folded piece of oilskin with a hand-drawn map — not of the mine, but of a route through the mountains, marked with a red X near a place labeled "La Cueva del Oro" — the Cave of Gold. On the reverse, in hurried handwriting: "If I die, find Rosalita. She has the rest. The governor poisoned the well at Spider Rock. Prove it. — S.V."


                
// Valdez left a message for anyone who would look. The map is only half the story. Rosalita — whoever she is — has the other half. //

            

            

                
                
            

        


        
        

            

            // GALLOWS KNOLL — CHAOS AT DAWN //
            

                
Your revolver clears leather before you've fully thought it through. The shot severs the rope just as the trapdoor opens, and Valdez crashes through the gallows floor into the dust below — alive, gasping, but alive. The crowd scatters. The marshal's hand goes to his gun but hesitates. Governor Crane's face twists with fury. "Shoot them both!" he bellows, but his guards are slow to react, and in the confusion you manage to haul Valdez to his feet.


                
"Gracias," he chokes out. "I knew someone would come. My sister — Rosalita — she sent the letter. She's at the Silver Vein. But we can't stay here. The governor will have us both killed within the hour. We need to run — now. I know a place in the mountains where they won't find us. The gold is real. I can take you there. But you have to trust me."


                
Behind you, the marshal is shouting for order. Crane's guards are pushing through the panicked crowd. You have seconds to decide.


                
// You've just become an outlaw. Every lawman in the territory will be after you by noon. But Valdez is alive, and he knows where the treasure is buried. //

            

            

                
                
            

        


        
        

            

            // MARSHAL'S OFFICE — PINNED & SWORN //
            

                
Marshal Whitfield pins the tin star to your vest with hands that tremble slightly. "You're a deputy now," he says. "That gives you the right to ask questions. But it also puts a target on your back. Governor Crane doesn't like people poking around his affairs." He hands you a ring of keys and a worn ledger. "Start with the mine office. The Silver Queen Mine — that's where the tailings supposedly came from. See if the records match Valdez's story. While you're there, keep an eye out for anything that connects back to the governor."


                
He pauses at the door. "Oh — and watch your back at the mine. It's been closed since the poisoning was discovered. But Crane's men still patrol up there. Unofficially. They say they're protecting the site. I say they're hiding something."


                
Through the window, you can see the gallows on the distant knoll. The crowd is still gathered. The hanging hasn't happened yet. You have time.


                
// You're now the law in a town where the law has been bending toward corruption for months. The badge gives you access. It also makes you visible. //

            

            

                
                
            

        


        
        

            

            // MARSHAL'S OFFICE — THE APACHE ANGLE //
            

                
"Chief Nantan," Marshal Whitfield says, tracing a line on a territory map. "His band has lived in these mountains for generations — long before the Spanish came, long before the silver rush, long before anyone thought to draw borders on paper. They know every stream, every cave, every hidden valley. When the sickness started, Nantan came to me personally. Said the water at Spider Rock had been poisoned — deliberately. Said his people had seen Crane's men dumping barrels upstream."


                
The marshal rubs his eyes. "I wanted to investigate. But Crane got the territorial judge to issue a warrant for Nantan's arrest instead — 'inciting unrest,' they called it. Now the Apache won't talk to any white lawman. But you..." He looks at you appraisingly. "You're new. You're not carrying the baggage of the last six months. If you ride to their camp — alone, unarmed except for a peace offering — Nantan might speak with you. He might tell you what he saw. It's dangerous, but it might be the only way to get the truth before Valdez hangs."


                
// The Apache have been watching from the high country. They know something about the poisoned water. But approaching their camp is a risk — Crane has posted bounties on any Apache found off the reservation. //

            

            

                
                
            

        


        
        

            

            // SILVER VEIN SALOON — THE SISTER'S STORY //
            

                
You sit down across from the woman. Up close, she's younger than she first appeared — early twenties, with dark hair pulled back and eyes that have been crying recently but aren't crying now. "You got my letter," she says quietly. "I'm Rosalita Valdez. Santiago is my brother. And he's going to die in..." she glances at the window, "...less than an hour, unless someone does something."


                
She slides a worn leather journal across the table. "This is my brother's mining log. He kept detailed records of every vein he found, every assay, every conversation. Six weeks ago, he discovered something at Spider Rock — not just silver. Gold. Spanish gold, hidden in a cave behind a waterfall. He told Governor Crane, thinking he'd get a fair partnership. Instead, Crane had the well poisoned, blamed Santiago, and pushed for a hanging. He wants the gold for himself — all of it."


                
She opens the journal to a marked page. It shows a geological survey with coordinates and a hand-drawn route. "The gold is in a cave called La Cueva del Oro. My brother found it. He's the only one who knows exactly where it is. But Crane doesn't know that Santiago already gave me the location. If we can prove the poisoning was Crane's doing, we can save my brother. If we can't..." She closes the journal. "Then at least we can stop Crane from getting the gold."


                
// Rosalita is brave, desperate, and carrying the weight of her family's survival. She has the map. She has the journal. She needs an ally. //

            

            

                
                
            

        


        
        

            

            // SILVER VEIN SALOON — HIGH STAKES AT DAWN //
            

                
You slide into the empty chair at the poker table. The three soldiers — Sergeant Brock, Corporal Hayes, and a private who looks barely old enough to shave — eye you with the wariness of men who've been losing money all night. "Buy-in's five dollars," Brock says. His broken nose gives his voice a nasal quality. "You got the stomach for it, stranger?"


                
The cards are dealt. The whiskey flows. And as the game progresses, the soldiers get looser with their words. Brock complains about "babysitting that damned mine entrance for two weeks straight." Hayes mutters about "barrels in the wagon — heavy ones, marked as mining supplies but they weren't." The private says nothing, but his eyes dart nervously whenever the governor's name comes up.


                
On the bar beside their chips sits a leather journal — not Rosalita's, but a territorial record book with Crane's seal embossed on the cover. One of them brought it in and forgot to secure it. If you win this hand, you could demand the journal as part of the pot. If you lose, you'll have to find another way.


                
// The soldiers know more than they're saying. The journal could be the key to everything. But Sergeant Brock doesn't look like a man who loses gracefully. //

            

            

                
                
            

        


        
        

            

            // MOUNTAIN TRAIL — FOLLOWING VALDEZ'S MAP //
            

                
The map leads you away from Crow's Perch, up a narrow deer trail that switchbacks through dense pine forest. The air grows colder as you climb. The only sounds are your horse's hooves on the pine needles and the distant rush of a waterfall somewhere ahead. The map's red X is marked near a geological feature labeled "Spider Rock — waterfall — cave behind."


                
After two hours of riding, you find it: a narrow canyon where a waterfall tumbles forty feet into a clear pool. And behind the cascade, barely visible through the spray, is a dark opening in the rock face — La Cueva del Oro. But you're not alone. Fresh boot prints lead toward the cave entrance. Someone else found this place — recently. And from the look of the prints, they came in numbers.


                
Near the pool, you spot an empty barrel with territorial government markings. The same kind of barrel, you'd wager, that was used to carry the tailings that poisoned the town well. A torn piece of cloth snagged on a branch bears a button with Crane's insignia.


                
// The evidence is mounting. Governor Crane poisoned the well. Valdez found the gold. And now Crane's men are inside the cave — possibly right now. //

            

            

                
                
            

        


        
        

            

            // MAIN STREET — THE GOVERNOR'S CARRIAGE //
            

                
You catch Governor Crane's carriage just as it pulls away from the gallows. He rolls down the window with the expression of a man who is not accustomed to being stopped in the street. "I'm busy," he says flatly, but his eyes flick to the oilskin map visible in your hand. Something shifts in his expression — a tightening around the mouth, a cooling of his already-cold gaze.


                
"That's Valdez's map," he says. "You'd be wise to hand it over. It's territorial property now — evidence in a poisoning case. Possession of stolen evidence is a crime, you know." He smiles, but it doesn't reach his eyes. "I could have you arrested. But I'm a reasonable man. Give me the map, and I'll forget we ever had this conversation. Hell, I might even find a reward for your cooperation."


                
Behind him, through the carriage window, you glimpse papers on the seat — a geological survey, a telegram from a railroad company in Denver, and a document marked "Land Acquisition — Spider Rock Valley." The governor isn't just after the gold. He's after the entire valley. The railroad wants a route through these mountains, and Crane is clearing the way.


                
// Crane is dangerous not because he's violent — though he certainly can be — but because he's patient. He's been planning this for months. Maybe years. //

            

            

                
                
            

        


        
        

            

            // MOUNTAIN PASS — FUGITIVES AT LARGE //
            

                
You and Valdez ride hard into the mountains, leaving the chaos of Crow's Perch behind. By noon, you've climbed high enough that the pines give way to aspens, their leaves whispering silver-green in the wind. Valdez knows these trails well — he's been prospecting here for years. "The cave is another day's ride," he says. "But we need to be careful. Crane has patrols everywhere. And there's something else..." He hesitates. "The Apache. Chief Nantan's people. They know about the gold. They've known for centuries. They consider the cave sacred. If we go in there, we might have to answer to them as well."


                
You make camp in a sheltered hollow as the sun sets. Valdez, finally free of the noose, talks more openly. "The gold isn't just Spanish," he explains. "It's older than that. The conquistadors took it from the Pueblo people, who took it from... someone else. There are carvings in the cave — things I couldn't read. The Apache call it 'the place where the sky fell.' They say the gold is cursed. I didn't believe it at first. But after what happened — the sickness, the hanging — I'm not so sure."


                
In the distance, a wolf howls. The stars are impossibly bright at this altitude.


                
// You're an outlaw now, riding with a condemned man toward a cursed treasure. The governor's men are behind you. The Apache are ahead. And the gold is somewhere in between. //

            

            

                
                
            

        


        
        

            

            // SILVER VEIN SALOON — BACK ROOM — HIDDEN IN PLAIN SIGHT //
            

                
You drag Valdez through the back alleys of Crow's Perch and into the saloon's storage room. Rosalita, summoned by the bartender, arrives within minutes. The reunion between brother and sister is brief but fierce — whispered words in Spanish, a tight embrace, and then a shared look of determination. "We have maybe until nightfall before they search every building," Rosalita says. "But I've been gathering evidence for weeks. Testimonies from miners who saw Crane's men at the well. A torn uniform with tailings residue. And this..."


                
She produces a crumpled telegram from her pocket. It's from the Denver & Rio Grande Railroad to Governor Crane, dated three months ago: "Route through Spider Rock Valley approved. Land acquisition critical. Clear all obstacles by year's end. Bonus for early completion — $15,000."


                
"The railroad is paying him," Rosalita says. "He poisoned the well to drive people away from Spider Rock. When Santiago found the gold, Crane realized he could kill two birds with one stone — eliminate the only witness who knew about the poisoning AND claim the treasure for himself. He's not just corrupt. He's a murderer."


                
// You now have the evidence to prove Crane's crimes. But presenting it publicly means revealing Valdez is alive — and bringing the governor's full wrath down on everyone in this room. //

            

            

                
                
            

        


        
        

            

            // SILVER QUEEN MINE — CLOSED BUT NOT ABANDONED //
            

                
The Silver Queen Mine sits above town like a wound in the mountainside — dark tunnels, rusting equipment, and a foreboding silence broken only by the wind. The mine office is a small wooden shack with a padlocked door. Your deputy keys open it easily.


                
Inside, the records tell a clear story. Valdez's mining logs show he discovered a promising silver vein near Spider Rock six weeks ago and filed all the proper paperwork. But subsequent entries — written in a different hand — describe "contamination of the primary well" and name Valdez as the responsible party. The handwriting matches samples you've seen of Governor Crane's correspondence. He doctored the records.


                
Deeper in the office, you find a locked cabinet. Inside: a set of geological survey maps showing the Spider Rock valley, a letter from the railroad offering to purchase the land "once current occupants are removed," and a small glass vial labeled "Silver Tailings — Concentrate — HANDLE WITH CARE." The vial is half-empty. A laboratory report clipped to it confirms: "Substance is water-soluble and highly toxic when ingested. Symptoms consistent with reported illness in Crow's Perch."


                
// This is the smoking gun. Crane's men used concentrated mine tailings to poison the well. Valdez was framed. The evidence is here — but getting it out of the mine without being caught is another matter. //

            

            

                
                
            

        


        
        

            

            // CRANE'S RENTED MANSION — EDGE OF TOWN — THE GOVERNOR'S SECRETS //
            

                
The mansion is a two-story Victorian that looks wildly out of place in a mining town — imported furniture, velvet curtains, and a study filled with papers. With the governor occupied at the gallows, the house is lightly guarded. You slip through a side window and find yourself in his private study.


                
The desk is covered in documents. A map of the Spider Rock valley with proposed railroad routes drawn in red ink. A contract with the Denver & Rio Grande Railroad, promising "exclusive right-of-way through the Sangre de Cristo range." And most damning of all — a personal journal in Crane's handwriting, describing his plan in chilling detail:


                
"The well at Spider Rock has been treated. The sickness will be blamed on Valdez — his proximity to the mine makes him an easy scapegoat. Once the town is sufficiently frightened, I will push for a swift execution. Afterward, the land can be purchased for pennies. The railroad will pay handsomely. And if the rumors of Spanish gold are true... well. A governor's salary is modest. A man must plan for his retirement."


                
A floorboard creaks behind you. One of Crane's guards has returned early.


                
// You have the evidence in your hands. But getting out of this mansion alive is going to require either a very good explanation or a very fast exit. //

            

            

                
                
            

        


        
        

            

            // APACHE CAMP — HIGH COUNTRY — NANTAN'S FIRE //
            

                
You ride into the Apache camp with your hands visible and your rifle left behind. It's a gamble, but it pays off — Chief Nantan, an older man with a face like weathered stone, receives you with cautious dignity. His people have been watching the town for weeks. They know about the sickness. They know about Valdez. They know more than you expected.


                
"The white governor poisoned the water," Nantan says, his English accented but clear. "We saw his men carrying barrels to Spider Rock. We saw them dump the poison into the stream. We tried to warn the marshal, but the governor called us liars and put a bounty on our heads." He stirs the fire. "Now you come asking for truth. Why should I trust you?"


                
You tell him about Valdez, about the map, about the gold. At the mention of La Cueva del Oro, Nantan's expression changes. "That cave is sacred," he says quietly. "The gold inside does not belong to the Spanish, or the white miners, or the railroad. It belongs to the spirits of this mountain. If you go in there seeking wealth, the mountain will claim you. But if you go in seeking justice..." He pauses. "Then perhaps the spirits will allow it."


                
He offers you a choice: "I will take you to the cave myself. But you must promise that the gold will not be stolen — it will be used to help the people this governor has harmed."


                
// Nantan is offering an alliance. But it means promising to use the gold for justice, not personal gain. //

            

            

                
                
            

        


        
        

            

            // SPIDER ROCK — THE POISONED STREAM //
            

                
You ride to Spider Rock alone — a towering sandstone spire that marks the headwaters of the stream that feeds Crow's Perch. The Apache are right. Near the base of the rock, you find the remains of a campsite: empty barrels with territorial markings, a broken wagon wheel, and dark stains on the ground where something was spilled. The stream water here has an unnatural sheen — iridescent, faintly metallic. You wouldn't drink it. Neither should anyone else.


                
As you document the evidence, you hear horses approaching. A patrol of Crane's men — six of them, armed and moving fast. They must have spotted your trail. You have two choices: hide and let them pass, or ambush them and try to capture one for interrogation. The terrain favors the defender — narrow canyon walls, plenty of cover, and the rushing water to mask the sound of movement.


                
Through your spyglass, you recognize the lead rider: Sergeant Brock, the man from the saloon poker game. He's carrying something — a leather satchel stuffed with papers. Crane's orders, perhaps. Or something else.


                
// Six against one are poor odds. But Brock is carrying something important, and you might not get another chance to intercept it. //

            

            

                
                
            

        


        
        

            

            // MOUNTAIN TRAIL — ROSALITA'S MAP //
            

                
With Rosalita's journal in hand, you ride toward Spider Rock and the hidden cave. The journal is more than a map — it's a record of Santiago's weeks of exploration, filled with notes about the terrain, the wildlife, and the strange carvings he found in the cave. "There are symbols here I don't recognize," he wrote. "Older than Spanish. Older than Pueblo. Like nothing I've ever seen."


                
The trail is steep and narrow. As you climb, you become aware that you're being watched. Shapes move among the trees — not threatening, but present. Apache scouts, probably, tracking your progress toward the sacred site. They don't interfere. But their presence is a reminder that this land has guardians older than any claim Governor Crane or the railroad might make.


                
You reach the waterfall by mid-afternoon. The cave entrance behind the cascade glows faintly — a trick of the light, or something else? The journal mentions that the gold inside "seems to generate its own warmth." You're about to find out what that means.


                
// The cave awaits. Inside: gold, secrets, and possibly a truth that changes everything. //

            

            

                
                
            

        


        
        

            

            // GALLOWS KNOLL — A PUBLIC ACCUSATION //
            

                
You push through the crowd just as the marshal is about to pull the lever. "Stop!" you shout, holding Rosalita's journal above your head. "I have evidence that Santiago Valdez is innocent. Governor Crane poisoned the well himself. It's all in this journal — and I have witnesses who will testify."


                
The crowd stirs. Marshal Whitfield steps forward, his expression a mixture of relief and dread. Governor Crane's face turns the color of old milk. "This is absurd," he sputters. "Arrest this man for interfering with a lawful execution!" But his guards hesitate. The crowd is growing restless. Some have lost children to the sickness. They want answers — real answers.


                
Rosalita steps out from the crowd, her voice clear and steady. "My brother is innocent. The governor offered to spare Santiago's life if I gave him the location of the gold. When I refused, he moved the hanging up by a week. He's not interested in justice. He's interested in covering up his crimes."


                
Murmurs turn to shouts. The marshal looks at Crane, then at the journal, then at the crowd. "I'm postponing this hanging," he announces, "pending a full investigation."


                
// You've just publicly accused the most powerful man in the territory of murder. If you can't prove it, you'll hang alongside Valdez. //

            

            

                
                
            

        


        
        

            

            // SILVER VEIN SALOON — WINNING HAND //
            

                
You lay down a full house. Sergeant Brock stares at the cards like they've personally betrayed him. "Damn," he mutters. "Fine. Take the pot. Take the damned journal too — I was supposed to deliver it to the governor hours ago. He's going to have my hide." He shoves the leather-bound book across the table and storms out, his men trailing behind him.


                
The journal is a goldmine — metaphorically speaking. It contains Crane's orders to his men over the past three months: instructions to poison the Spider Rock well, to frame Valdez, to intimidate anyone who asked too many questions. It also contains a detailed map of the Spider Rock valley with the location of La Cueva del Oro clearly marked — Crane has known about the gold all along. He wasn't just poisoning a well. He was clearing the land for a railroad AND stealing a treasure. The well poisoning was a convenient way to accomplish both.


                
The bartender, who has been watching the whole exchange, slides you a key. "Back room," he says quietly. "Rosalita Valdez is waiting there. She's been gathering evidence for weeks. With what you've got, you might actually have a chance to bring Crane down."


                
// You have Crane's orders in your hands. Combined with Rosalita's evidence, this could end the governor's reign of corruption. //

            

            

                
                
            

        


        
        

            

            // SILVER VEIN SALOON — FOLDING HAND //
            

                
You fold. Sergeant Brock grins, rakes in the pot, and pockets the journal. "Smart man," he says. "Not everyone knows when to quit." He and his men leave the saloon, headed toward the governor's mansion. But as they go, you notice something: the private — the young one who barely spoke — drops a folded piece of paper near your chair. Intentional or accidental? Either way, you retrieve it.


                
The note reads: "Midnight. The old stamp mill above the mine. I'll tell you everything. I joined up for money but I didn't sign up to poison children. — PFC Thomas."


                
You now have a potential informant inside Crane's operation. The private is young, scared, and apparently burdened by a conscience. Meeting him at the stamp mill is a risk — it could be a trap. But if he's genuine, he could provide firsthand testimony that no journal or document could match.


                
// A soldier with a guilty conscience is a fragile but valuable asset. If you can get him to talk, you might not need the journal at all. //

            

            

                
                
            

        


        
        

            

            // LA CUEVA DEL ORO — BEHIND THE WATERFALL //
            

                
You step through the waterfall and into a vast limestone cavern. For a moment, the darkness is complete — then your eyes adjust, and you realize the cave is not dark at all. Veins of phosphorescent mineral run through the walls, casting a soft blue-green glow over everything. And in the center of the cavern, piled on a natural stone platform, is the gold.


                
It's more than you imagined. Spanish doubloons, conquistador armor, jeweled crucifixes, and older artifacts — Pueblo turquoise, Aztec gold worked into intricate patterns, and stranger things still. Carvings on the walls depict beings that are neither human nor animal, their outlines picked out in the glowing mineral. The air is warm and still, and you feel a strange pressure behind your eyes — not unpleasant, but present. Watching.


                
But you're not the first to arrive. Governor Crane is here — he must have ridden ahead while his men created a diversion at the gallows. He stands before the gold, his back to you, a revolver in his hand. "I was wondering when someone would figure it out," he says without turning around. "Valdez talked, didn't he?
