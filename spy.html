The briefing file is stamped in red: MOST SECRET — DESTROY AFTER READING. You read it twice, then burn it in the hotel ashtray, just like they taught you. The ashes smell like trouble.

Your name, for the purposes of this operation, is Klaus Bergmann — West German antiquarian, traveling to East Berlin on a cultural exchange visa to examine pre-war manuscripts at the Humboldt University library. Your real name, your real employer, your real purpose: all of that is filed in a locked cabinet in Vauxhall Cross, London, and it stays there.

The actual mission: OPERATION BROKEN MIRROR. Three weeks ago, a senior KGB cryptographer named Aleksandr Volkov made contact with British intelligence through an intermediary in Vienna. Volkov claims to hold — in his head, not on paper — the complete algorithmic structure of SOVA-7, the Soviet cipher system currently protecting all Warsaw Pact military communications. SOVA-7 replaced the old system six months ago. Since then, GCHQ has been deaf and blind to Soviet military traffic. If Volkov's claim is genuine, SOVA-7 could be broken within weeks. The intelligence implications are staggering.

The problem: Volkov is having second thoughts. He wants guarantees London can't put in writing. He wants a face-to-face meeting with someone who can make promises. Someone with authority. Your station chief, Edith Marsh, decided that someone is you.

The second problem: your primary contact in East Berlin, an asset code-named SPARROW, has gone silent. Three missed signals. Either SPARROW has been rolled up by the Stasi, or SPARROW is compromised and waiting to hand you over. You don't know which. You have to find out before you can make contact with Volkov — because SPARROW was the only person who knew Volkov's location and schedule.

You crossed at Checkpoint Charlie this morning. Your papers are clean. Your cover is solid. Your handler, a cigarette-voiced woman in the Trade Mission building on Unter den Linden, slipped you a folded note with your safehouses and dead-drop locations. Then she walked away without looking back, which is either professionalism or a very bad sign.

You're standing on the corner of Karl-Marx-Allee, East Berlin, with a briefcase full of plausible manuscripts, a concealed 9mm Walther you'd rather not use, and approximately four hours before the Stasi begin their evening shift-change — the brief window when surveillance gaps open up and things can happen without official notice.

Your note listed three starting points. Choose carefully. In East Berlin, the wrong door is the last one you open.
