            // CARPATHIAN FOOTHILLS — NIGHT OF THE HUNTER'S MOON //
            

                
The castle rises against the moon like a blade held to the sky. You've been walking since dusk, following the old road that winds through the Carpathian foothills — a road the villagers back home swore was cursed. "Nobody who goes to Castle Sanguine comes back the same," they said. "Most don't come back at all."


                
But your village is dying. The Crimson Plague has taken thirty-seven souls this winter alone. The healers have tried everything. The priests have prayed. Nothing works. Then Elder Vasile, on his deathbed, whispered about the castle — about a relic hidden within, the Heart of Sanguine, a crystalline object said to purify blood and cure any ailment. He described it in detail before the fever took him: a fist-sized gem the color of fresh arterial blood, warm to the touch, kept in the highest tower behind a door that only opens under moonlight.


                
You volunteered. Someone had to. Now, standing before the iron gates with your lantern flickering in the mountain wind, you're not sure whether you were brave or just desperate.


                
The castle is enormous — black stone, gothic spires, narrow windows that glow faintly from within. The front gate is slightly ajar, rust flaking from the hinges. To the left, ivy climbs the wall toward a balconied window on the second floor. To the right, a low stone wall encloses what appears to be an overgrown garden, its iron gate hanging loose.


                
// Three ways in. Each holds its own peril. Choose quickly — the moon is rising, and something inside this castle knows you're here. //

            

            

                
                
                
            

        


        
        

            

            // CASTLE SANGUINE — GREAT HALL — THE HOUR OF WELCOME //
            

                
The main gate groans as you push it open, the sound echoing through stone corridors like a long-held breath finally released. Beyond is the great hall — vast, arched ceilings lost in shadow, tall windows draped in burgundy velvet, and a long dining table set for one. Candles flicker in iron sconces, their flames a warm amber that feels almost inviting after the cold climb up the mountain.


                
At the far end of the table, a figure rises from a high-backed chair. He is tall, lean, dressed in a black velvet coat with silver buttons. His face is pale — not the pallor of illness, but the polished white of marble. His eyes are dark and deep-set, and when he smiles, it doesn't quite reach them. "A guest," he says, his voice smooth as old silk. "How rare. How delightful. Please — sit. Eat. You must be exhausted." He gestures to the table, where a covered platter steams faintly.


                
Behind him, partially concealed by a curtain, you glimpse a narrow stone staircase spiraling upward. A faint reddish glow pulses from somewhere above — rhythmic, like a heartbeat made visible.


                
// The man has not introduced himself. The food smells incredible. The glow from upstairs is exactly the color Elder Vasile described. //

            

            

                
                
            

        


        
        

            

            // CASTLE SANGUINE — UPPER CHAMBER — THE PRISONER'S WINDOW //
            

                
The ivy is thick and ancient — your hands find purchase easily, and within a few tense minutes you're pulling yourself onto a stone balcony. The window beyond is cracked open, lace curtains stirring in the cold breeze. You slip inside.


                
The chamber is unexpectedly warm. A fire crackles in a marble hearth. Bookshelves line one wall, filled with leather-bound volumes in languages you don't recognize. And against the far wall, chained by one ankle to an iron ring set in the stone, sits a young woman in a simple gray dress. Her hair is dark, her eyes are tired, and when she sees you, she flinches — then leans forward with desperate hope.


                
"Please," she whispers, "you have to get me out of here. The key is on the mantle. Please — he'll be back soon. He's not what he seems. None of this is what it seems." She points to a small iron key resting on the fireplace mantle. "My name is Anna. I was a traveler, like you. He's kept me here for months. Please."


                
Through the chamber door, you hear footsteps — slow, deliberate, ascending stone stairs. You have maybe two minutes.


                
// Her story makes sense. But there's something about the way she watches you — too focused, too calm for someone who's been imprisoned for months. //

            

            

                
                
            

        


        
        

            

            // CASTLE SANGUINE — OVERGROWN GARDEN — THE ROSE COURT //
            

                
The garden gate swings open with a sound like a whispered complaint. Inside, the garden has gone wild — rose bushes grown into tangled walls, their blooms a deep, unsettling red that looks almost black in the moonlight. The air is thick with their scent, cloying and sweet, layered over something earthier beneath. Stone benches sit half-buried in moss. A dry fountain stands at the center, its basin cracked.


                
At the far end of the garden, a stone staircase descends into the earth — the family crypt, its entrance framed by two weathered statues of hooded figures with clasped hands. Beside it, a smaller building with a chimney — the servants' quarters — leans tiredly against the outer wall, its door hanging open.


                
You notice that the roses nearest the crypt are different — they glow very faintly, a soft red luminescence that pulses in time with something you can't quite feel. The same rhythmic pattern the Elder described when he spoke of the Heart of Sanguine.


                
// The crypt promises answers. The servants' quarters promise information. The roses promise nothing but secrets. //

            

            

                
                
            

        


        
        

            

            // GREAT HALL — THE FEAST //
            

                
You sit. The stranger's smile deepens. "Wise," he murmurs, and pours you a glass of wine so dark it's nearly black. "I am Count Mircalla Sanguine. This castle has been my family's home for — well. A very long time." He doesn't specify how long. The wine tastes of blackberries and something metallic, like iron or old blood.


                
As you eat, he talks. About the castle's history, about the valley below, about the plague that's been sweeping through the villages. He mentions it casually, the way one might mention the weather, but his dark eyes watch your reaction with unsettling intensity. "The Crimson Plague is a tragedy," he says. "But not a natural one. Someone designed it. Someone who wanted to test whether a population under extreme duress would produce — interesting results." He swirls his wine. "I know who. And I know where the cure is kept."


                
He leans forward. "The Heart of Sanguine. It's real. It's in my tower. I can give it to you — but I need something in return. A small thing. A vial of your blood. Just one. For my... research."


                
// The Count's eyes have not blinked once during this entire conversation. //

            

            

                
                
            

        


        
        

            

            // GREAT HALL — THE REFUSAL //
            

                
"I'd rather not eat," you say, keeping your voice steady. "But I'd appreciate information. I'm looking for something."


                
The stranger's smile freezes for just a moment — a flicker of something cold passing behind his eyes — then returns. "Of course," he says, though his tone has cooled. "You're here for the Heart. They always are." He sets down his goblet and rises. "I won't stop you from seeking it. The tower door is at the top of the staircase behind me. It opens only under direct moonlight, which you'll find streaming through the upper window in approximately twelve minutes." He pauses. "But I should warn you — the Heart is guarded. Not by me. By something older than me, and considerably less polite."


                
He gestures toward a side door. "If you want to understand what you're walking into, visit my library first. First door on the left. The records there go back three hundred years. They'll tell you everything you need to know about the Heart — and about me."


                
// He's being unusually helpful for someone who clearly doesn't want you here. Either he's confident you'll fail, or he's testing you. //

            

            

                
                
            

        


        
        

            

            // UPPER CHAMBER — THE UNCHAINING //
            

                
You grab the key and unlock the shackle. Anna gasps, rubbing her ankle, and stands on unsteady legs. "Thank you," she breathes. "Thank you — we don't have much time. He'll be here any minute."


                
She leads you through a servant's corridor behind the bookcase — a narrow passage you never would have found alone — and down a spiraling stairwell into the bowels of the castle. "The Heart of Sanguine is real," she says as you descend. "But the Count lied about what it does. It doesn't just cure the plague. It creates it. The plague is his design — a modified version of an older disease, something alchemical. He spreads it, then waits for desperate people to come looking for the cure. And when they do..." She stops at a heavy oak door. "He offers them a choice. Their blood for the Heart. But the Heart doesn't cure anything. It just makes them carriers. They go home, the plague stops in their village, and starts in the next one. He's been doing it for centuries."


                
She turns to face you. Her eyes are strange in the torchlight — too reflective, almost luminous. "The real cure is in the crypt," she says. "But you'll need me to get past the guardian. I can help you — if you trust me."


                
// She knows an awful lot for someone who was supposedly chained up for months. But her information matches too many details to be entirely false. //

            

            

                
                
            

        


        
        

            

            // UPPER CHAMBER — TRUST YOUR INSTINCTS //
            

                
You leave the key on the mantle and instead examine the room. Anna's expression shifts — the desperate hope flickers, replaced by something colder. "You're making a mistake," she says, but her voice is different now, lower, and the chains rattle as she shifts her weight in a way that doesn't look uncomfortable at all.


                
You search the bookshelves and find a journal — handwritten, in elegant script. It details the castle's residents: Count Mircalla Sanguine, his sister Lady Annelise, and their retainers. The journal is dated 1743. A pressed rose falls from its pages, perfectly preserved, the same deep crimson as the ones blooming in the garden outside.


                
A bookmark notes: "Annelise was bitten in the spring of 1739. She retains her faculties but cannot leave the castle. She hungers. The Count keeps her in the upper chamber during daylight hours for her own protection — and for the protection of visitors. She is not dangerous unless freed by moonlight. She is very dangerous then."


                
The footsteps in the hallway are closer now. Anna — Annelise — is smiling at you from her chains, and it's not a grateful smile. It's the smile of something that has been waiting a very long time for someone to make the wrong choice.


                
// You made the right call. Now you need to get out of this room before the Count — or his sister — decides you've overstayed your welcome. //

            

            

                
                
            

        


        
        

            

            // CASTLE SANGUINE — FAMILY CRYPT — BELOW THE ROSE COURT //
            

                
The crypt stairs are worn smooth by centuries of footsteps. At the bottom, a heavy stone chamber opens before you — lined with sarcophagi, each carved with the Sanguine family crest: a rose with a dagger through its stem. Candles burn in alcoves, though you can't imagine who lit them. The air is cold and smells of old stone and dried flowers.


                
At the center of the crypt, on a raised stone bier, lies a sarcophagus larger than the others — the lid carved with the likeness of a woman in medieval dress, her hands folded over her chest, a serene expression on her stone face. At her feet, a small iron chest sits unopened. The lock is shaped like a rose.


                
An inscription on the sarcophagus reads: "Here lies Lenora Sanguine, First of the Line. She gave her blood that others might live. She gave her life that the Rose might bloom. The cure she carried sleeps with her still."


                
The iron chest at her feet pulses with that same faint red glow you saw in the garden roses — slow, rhythmic, like a sleeping heartbeat.


                
// Lenora Sanguine. The first vampire of this line. And according to this inscription, she carried the cure — not the plague. The story is more complicated than the villagers knew. //

            

            

                
                
            

        


        
        

            

            // CASTLE SANGUINE — SERVANTS' QUARTERS — ABANDONED BUT NOT EMPTY //
            

                
The servants' quarters are small and sparse — a kitchen, a pantry, two sleeping rooms. Everything is covered in a fine layer of dust, but there are signs of recent use: a half-burned candle, a bowl of water still clean, a wool blanket folded neatly on a cot. Someone lives here. Or something.


                
On the kitchen table, you find a letter, written in a shaky hand. It's addressed to no one — or perhaps to anyone who might find it:


                
"To whomever reads this — My name was Grigore. I served the Sanguine family for forty-seven years. The Count is not evil. He is cursed, as all his line are cursed, but he has spent three centuries trying to undo what his ancestor Lenora created. The Heart of Sanguine is not a relic — it is a prison. It contains the original strain of the plague, bound in crystal so it could never be released. The Count keeps it locked in the tower to protect the world from it. The plague in the villages is not his doing — it is the work of his sister Annelise, who was turned against her will and has spent two hundred years trying to perfect and spread the disease in revenge. The Count keeps her chained in the upper chamber. He is not your enemy. Annelise is. Take this information and use it wisely. — G."


                
At the bottom of the letter, a key is pressed into the paper with wax — a small iron key labeled TOWER.


                
// Grigore's letter changes everything. The Count is a jailer, not a monster. Annelise is the threat. And the Heart is a prison for the plague, not a cure for it. //

            

            

                
                
            

        


        
        

            

            // GREAT HALL — THE COUNT'S BARGAIN ACCEPTED //
            

                
You let the Count draw a small vial of blood from your arm. The process is quick, almost clinical — he uses a silver lancet and a glass vial, handling both with the practiced ease of someone who has done this many, many times before. "Thank you," he says, and for a moment he sounds almost sincere. "You'd be surprised how few people agree. Most would rather fight, or run, or try to steal the Heart without understanding what it is."


                
He leads you up the spiral staircase. The red glow intensifies with every step, and you feel it now — a pressure behind your eyes, a warmth in your chest. At the top, a heavy stone door stands open, moonlight pouring through a circular window directly onto a crystal pedestal. On the pedestal rests the Heart of Sanguine — exactly as Elder Vasile described it, a fist-sized gem the color of arterial blood, warm to the touch, pulsing with its own inner light.


                
"Take it," the Count says. "It will cure your village. But you should know — you will become a carrier. Not of the plague. Of the cure. Wherever you go, the plague will recede. But you will never be able to stay anywhere for long, because people will notice. People will ask questions. The life of a wandering healer is lonely." He pauses. "I should know. I've been doing it for three hundred years."


                
// The Count isn't what you expected. He's not a monster — he's a man carrying an impossible burden. And now he's offering you the same burden. //

            

            

                
                
            

        


        
        

            

            // CASTLE SANGUINE — THE LIBRARY — THREE HUNDRED YEARS OF RECORDS //
            

                
The library is a scholar's dream and a mortal's nightmare — shelves stretching up into darkness, ladders on rails, the smell of old leather and older paper. You light a candle and begin reading.


                
The Sanguine family history unfolds across centuries. Lenora Sanguine, the matriarch, was an alchemist and physician in the 1600s. When a plague swept through the Carpathians — an earlier, more lethal version of the Crimson Plague — she experimented on herself with a combination of alchemical compounds and something the records call "the sanguine infusion," derived from a rare mineral found only in these mountains. She cured herself and became immune — but the infusion changed her. Extended her life. Made her sensitive to sunlight. Gave her a thirst for blood that she fought for decades before finding a way to control it.


                
The Heart of Sanguine, you learn, is a crystalized form of her original cure — but bound with a containment spell that also holds the original plague strain inside it. It's both cure and disease, locked together in eternal stalemate. If the crystal is broken, the plague is released at full strength. If it's used correctly — placed in water, dissolved over seven days — it creates a true cure with no side effects.


                
You also find a letter from Count Mircalla to his ancestors, dated 1821: "I have contained Annelise. She will not harm another village. I will maintain the tower, guard the Heart, and wait for someone who can end this properly — someone who seeks the truth, not just the cure."


                
// The Count has been waiting two hundred years for someone to read this letter. //

            

            

                
                
            

        


        
        

            

            // CORRIDOR — THE MASK FALLS //
            

                
"Who am I?" Anna — Annelise — laughs, and it's not a pleasant sound. "I'm the one who's been trying to save you. But fine. You want the truth?"


                
She steps back, and the torchlight catches her face differently — older, sharper, with something ancient moving behind her eyes. "My brother Mircalla has been playing the tragic jailer for two centuries, and people keep falling for it. The Heart of Sanguine isn't a prison for the plague — it's a weapon. He uses it to spread the disease, then offers the 'cure' in exchange for blood samples. He's built an archive of human bloodlines spanning three hundred years. He's not a healer. He's a collector."


                
She pulls up her sleeve. Her arm is covered in small, neat scars — dozens of them, in various stages of healing. "He keeps me chained because I'm the only one who knows the truth and is willing to tell it. The real cure — the one Lenora actually made — is buried with her in the crypt. Mircalla destroyed all records of it except one, which I hid in the library. Under the loose floorboard, third shelf from the left, behind the red ledger."


                
She looks at you steadily. "You can still walk away. Go to the crypt, find Lenora's cure, leave the Heart where it is. Or you can go to Mircalla and let him spin you his version. But ask yourself — why would I be chained up if I were the dangerous one? What's easier to control — a prisoner, or a rival?"


                
// She makes a compelling argument. But so would anyone. You need to verify her claims independently. //

            

            

                
                
            

        


        
        

            

            // UPPER CHAMBER — A CALM CONVERSATION //
            

                
The door opens and Count Mircalla enters. He stops when he sees you standing by the bookshelf, journal in hand. His eyes move to Annelise — still chained, still watching with that cold smile — and something in his expression shifts. Not anger. Relief.


                
"You didn't free her," he says quietly. "In two hundred years, you're the first."


                
He crosses to the fireplace and adds a log. "My sister is very persuasive. She's had a long time to practice. The story she tells — that I'm the monster, that she's the victim, that the Heart is a weapon — it's a good story. Well-constructed. There's even truth in it, which is what makes it so effective." He sinks into a worn armchair. "The truth is messier. Annelise was turned against her will — that part is real. But the turning didn't just change her body. It changed her mind. She developed a kind of madness — a conviction that the only way to end her suffering was to spread it. She believes that if enough people carry the plague, someone will find a cure that works for everyone, including her. She's not evil. She's broken. And she's very, very dangerous."


                
He looks at you. "The Heart can cure your village. I'll give it to you freely — no blood bargain, no strings. The only thing I ask is that you hear the whole story before you leave. Lenora's story. Our family's story. It matters. It might matter more than you know."


                
// The Count is tired. Two hundred years of guarding his sister, maintaining the Heart, waiting for someone to listen. You might be the first person in centuries to actually hear him. //

            

            

                
                
            

        


        
        

            

            // FAMILY CRYPT — LENORA'S REST //
            

                
The sarcophagus lid is heavy but counterweighted — it swings open with surprising ease. Inside lies a woman preserved in an unnatural state between life and death. Her skin is pale but not decayed. Her eyes are closed. Her hands, folded over her chest, hold a small crystal vial filled with a glowing red liquid — brighter than the Heart, warmer in color, like liquid sunlight mixed with blood.


                
Her eyes open.


                
It happens slowly, dreamily, as if she's waking from a very long nap. She focuses on you with pupils that contract in the candlelight. "How long?" she whispers. Her voice is dry as old parchment. "How long has it been?"


                
You tell her: roughly three hundred years. She closes her eyes again briefly, then opens them. "I hoped it wouldn't be this long. I hoped someone would come sooner." She lifts the crystal vial slightly. "This is the true cure. Not the Heart — the Heart was my first attempt. It worked, but it carried the plague strain within it. This vial contains the perfected version. Seven drops in a village well, and the plague is gone forever — not suppressed, not carried elsewhere, just... gone. No side effects. No carriers. No curse."


                
Her hand trembles. "Take it. Please. I've been holding it for three centuries, waiting for someone who would use it properly. Don't let my children's mistakes define my legacy."


                
// Lenora Sanguine, the first vampire, is not a monster. She's a healer who made a flawed cure and spent her death waiting to make it right. //

            

            

                
                
            

        


        
        

            

            // FAMILY CRYPT — THE IRON CHEST //
            

                
The rose-shaped lock yields to pressure — it's not locked at all, merely closed. Inside the chest, you find a bundle of letters, a silver dagger, and a folded map of the castle. The letters are correspondence between Lenora and her children, dated from the 1690s through the early 1700s.


                
They tell a story of a family trying to contain a disaster. Lenora's cure worked — but the plague she'd created in the process of developing it was stored inside the Heart as a necessary part of the alchemical process. She couldn't destroy one without destroying the other. So she locked the Heart in the tower, entrusted the key to her eldest son, and tasked her descendants with a simple mission: wait. Wait for alchemical science to advance enough that the plague could be separated from the cure. Wait for someone who could finish what she started.


                
The last letter, written in a different hand, is from Count Mircalla: "Mother, I am still waiting. Annelise grows more unstable each year. I fear I will have to chain her for her own safety, and for the safety of others. I will maintain the tower, guard the Heart, and keep her contained. I hope someone reads this letter someday and understands — we are not monsters. We are caretakers of a mistake we have spent three centuries trying to fix."


                
At the bottom of the chest, wrapped in velvet, is a small crystal vial. The label reads: "Perfected Cure — L. Sanguine, 1712."


                
// The cure was here all along. Lenora perfected it before she died. Her children either didn't know about it, or forgot. //

            

            

                
            

        


        
        

            

            // CASTLE SANGUINE — THE TOWER — UNDER MOONLIGHT //
            

                
The tower door opens to moonlight, just as the Count said. The circular room at the top is small and spare — stone walls, a single window, and in the center, the Heart of Sanguine on its crystal pedestal. Up close, it's breathtaking — a gem that seems to contain an entire sunset inside it, swirling with deep reds and golds and the faintest hints of amber. It's warm. Almost alive.


                
The pedestal has an inscription carved around its base: "He who takes the Heart takes also its burden. The plague and the cure are one. To carry this stone is to carry both. Choose with full knowledge."


                
The moonlight intensifies, and for a moment the Heart seems to pulse in time with your own heartbeat. You feel it — the weight of the choice, the centuries of waiting, the village thirty-seven souls deep in grief and counting. Whatever you decide, it has to be now. The moon is at its zenith. The Heart is at its most receptive.


                
// You have everything you need to make this decision. The only question is which version of the truth you believe. //

            

            

                
                
            

        


        
        

            

            // CASTLE SANGUINE — CELLAR PASSAGE — BENEATH THE FOUNDATIONS //
            

                
The cellar passage is old — older than the castle above it, the stones rough-hewn and unmortared. This was here before the Sanguines built their fortress. This was here before anyone thought to build anything in these mountains. The air grows colder with each step, and the faint red glow intensifies, pulsing steadily through cracks in the stone.


                
You emerge into a natural cavern beneath the crypt. Stalactites hang overhead like stone teeth. A subterranean stream runs through the center, its water clear and cold and faintly luminous. And at the far end of the cavern, resting on a natural stone shelf, is a sarcophagus older than the ones in the crypt above — carved with symbols you don't recognize, older than any language you know.


                
This is the true resting place of Lenora Sanguine. Not the ornate tomb upstairs — that was a decoy, meant for visitors who might come looking. This is where the first of the Sanguine line chose to spend her eternity, guarding the perfected cure she made in the last years of her life. The air here is still. Sacred. Waiting.


                
The sarcophagus lid is carved with a single word: "SPERANTA" — hope, in the old tongue.


                
// You've found it. The true tomb. The real cure. Whatever happens next, you're standing at the heart of a three-hundred-year-old mystery, and the answer is within arm's reach. //

            

            

                
                
            

        


        
        

            

            // CASTLE SANGUINE — TOWER SPIRE — THE HIGHEST POINT //
            

                
You climb. The spiral staircase seems endless, winding up through the tower's core, each step bringing you closer to the rhythmic red glow that has become as familiar as your own pulse. The walls are lined with portraits — generations of Sanguines, pale-faced and dark-eyed, watching you climb with painted stares.


                
At the top, the tower room is exactly as described. The Heart pulses on its pedestal. Moonlight streams through the window. And standing beside the Heart, waiting for you, is the Count.


                
"I wondered if you'd make it here," he says. "Most don't. Most get lost in the library, or seduced by my sister's stories, or frightened away by the crypt. But you — you kept climbing." He steps aside, gesturing to the Heart. "It's yours. I won't stop you. I've been waiting two hundred years for someone to take it off my hands. But before you do — I want you to know that there's another option. A better one. One my mother perfected before she died, and hid in a place she knew her children would never look."


                
He holds out a hand. In his palm is a small iron key — the same one from the servants' letter, the same shape as the rose lock on the iron chest in the crypt. "The perfected cure. No plague. No burden. Just — healing. It's in the iron chest at my mother's feet. I was too proud to look for it. Too sure I knew best. Don't make my mistake."


                
// The Count is handing you the key to the real cure. He's also handing you three hundred years of regret, wrapped in iron. //

            

            

                
                
            

        


        
        

            

            // CASTLE SANGUINE — THE RELIC ROOM — HIDDEN CHAMBER //
            

                
Behind a tapestry in the library, you find a hidden door. Beyond it, a small chamber lined with shelves holding the Sanguine family's most precious possessions — not gold or jewels, but records. Journals. Letters. A lock of hair tied with a red ribbon. A child's drawing of a rose. The accumulated memory of a family that has lived in the shadow of a curse for ten generations.


                
On a central pedestal, in a glass case, rests Lenora's final journal. The last entry reads: "I have done it. After forty-seven years of work, I have separated the plague from the cure. The perfected formula is stable. It will cure any who drink from a well treated with seven drops, and it carries no residual infection. I have placed a vial of it in the iron chest in the crypt, and another — hidden — in the cavern below, where the stream runs clean. My children do not know about the second vial. I could not bring myself to tell them, for fear they would fight over it. Let fate decide who finds it. Let hope guide their hands."


                
Below the entry, in fresher ink, someone has written: "Found this today. Two hundred and thirteen years after she wrote it. I never knew. — M.S."


                
// The Count found this journal recently. Very recently. He's been carrying the weight of this knowledge for a short time, after centuries of believing the Heart was the only option. //

            

            

                
            

        


        
        

            

            // UPPER CHAMBER — THE FINAL CONFRONTATION //
            

                
You return to the upper chamber armed with knowledge. Annelise sees it in your face immediately. "You figured it out," she says. Not a question. "Which version did you believe? My brother's tragic jailer story, or mine?"


                
"Neither," you say. "I believe Lenora's."


                
Annelise goes still. For a long moment, she doesn't move, doesn't blink, doesn't seem to breathe. Then, very quietly: "Grandmother Lenora?"


                
"She perfected the cure," you tell her. "Before she died. She hid it because she knew her descendants would fight over it. The Heart was never the answer — it was a stopgap, and everyone who came after her treated it like the final solution. Including you. Including Mircalla. All of you have been fighting over the wrong thing for three hundred years."


                
Annelise's expression crumbles. The cold, predatory mask falls away, and underneath is just a woman — tired, ancient, impossibly sad. "I didn't know," she whispers. "All this time. All those villages. I thought — I thought I was punishing him. I thought if I spread the plague, he'd have to admit the Heart was a lie. I never..." She covers her face with her hands. "What have I done?"


                
// Annelise is a villain. She's also a victim. The two things are not mutually exclusive, and the weight of that complexity fills the room like smoke. //

            

            

                
                
            

        


        
        

            

            // CASTLE SANGUINE — THE FLIGHT //
            

                
You run. Through the servant's passage, down the spiraling stairs, through the kitchen and into the cold night air of the garden. The roses seem to turn toward you as you pass, their crimson blooms tracking your movement like silent faces. Behind you, the castle looms dark and patient, its windows flickering with candlelight.
